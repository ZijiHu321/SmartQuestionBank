Question:
A particle moves along a straight line. Its displacement, $s$ metres, at time $t$ seconds is given by $s = t + \cos 2t$, $t \geq 0$. The first two times when the particle is at rest are denoted by $t_1$ and $t_2$, where $t_1 < t_2$.
(a) Find $t_1$ and $t_2$. [5]
(b) Find the displacement of the particle when $t = t_1$. [2]

Solution:
(a)
The particle is at rest when its velocity is zero. Velocity is the derivative of displacement with respect to time.
$v = \frac{ds}{dt} = \frac{d}{dt}(t + \cos 2t) = 1 - 2\sin 2t$ **M1**
Set $v = 0$:
$1 - 2\sin 2t = 0$ **M1**
$\Rightarrow \sin 2t = \frac{1}{2}$ **A1**
Solve for $t$:
$2t = \frac{\pi}{6}, \frac{5\pi}{6}, \frac{13\pi}{6}, \ldots$ (within the domain $t \geq 0$)
$t = \frac{\pi}{12}, \frac{5\pi}{12}, \frac{13\pi}{12}, \ldots$ **A1**
Thus, the first two times are:
$t_1 = \frac{\pi}{12}$ s, $t_2 = \frac{5\pi}{12}$ s **A1**

$\ $

(b)
Substitute $t_1 = \frac{\pi}{12}$ into the displacement equation:
$s = t + \cos 2t = \frac{\pi}{12} + \cos\left(2 \cdot \frac{\pi}{12}\right) = \frac{\pi}{12} + \cos\left(\frac{\pi}{6}\right)$ **M1**
$\cos\left(\frac{\pi}{6}\right) = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$
Therefore, $s = \frac{\pi}{12} + \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$ m **A1**

Question:
A body is moving in a straight line. When it is $s$ metres from a fixed point $O$ on the line its velocity, $v$, is given by $v = -\frac{1}{s^2}$, $s > 0$. Find the acceleration of the body when it is 50 cm from $O$. [6]

Solution:
First, find the derivative of velocity with respect to displacement:
$\frac{dv}{ds} = \frac{d}{ds}\left(-s^{-2}\right) = 2s^{-3}$ **M1A1**

Use the chain rule to find acceleration:
$a = v \frac{dv}{ds}$ **M1**
$a = -\frac{1}{s^2} \times \frac{2}{s^3} = -\frac{2}{s^5}$ **A1**

Convert 50 cm to metres: $s = 0.5$ m
Substitute $s = 0.5$ into the acceleration equation:
$a = -\frac{2}{(0.5)^5} = -\frac{2}{0.03125} = -64$ **M1A1**
The acceleration is $-64$ ms$^{-2}$.

(ii) Find expressions for the velocity, $v$, and the acceleration, $a$, of P. [4]
(iii) Determine all the solutions of the equation $v = 0$ for $0 < t < 4$. [5]

The period of $\sin(\pi t)$ is 2 seconds, and the period of $\sin(2\pi t)$ is 1 second. The fundamental period is the least common multiple, which is 2 seconds.
Period = 2 seconds **A1**

(ii)
Velocity is the derivative of displacement:
$v = \frac{ds}{dt} = 2\pi \cos(\pi t) + 2\pi \cos(2\pi t)$ **M1A1**
Acceleration is the derivative of velocity:
$a = \frac{dv}{dt} = -2\pi^2 \sin(\pi t) - 4\pi^2 \sin(2\pi t)$ **M1A1**

Set $v = 0$:
$2\pi (\cos(\pi t) + \cos(2\pi t)) = 0$

Use the identity $\cos(2\pi t) = 2\cos^2(\pi t) - 1$:
$\cos(\pi t) + 2\cos^2(\pi t) - 1 = 0$
$2\cos^2(\pi t) + \cos(\pi t) - 1 = 0$
Factorize:
$(2\cos(\pi t) - 1)(\cos(\pi t) + 1) = 0$ **A1**
Thus:
$\cos(\pi t) = \frac{1}{2}$ or $\cos(\pi t) = -1$ **A1**

$\pi t = \frac{\pi}{3}, \frac{5\pi}{3}, \frac{7\pi}{3}, \frac{11\pi}{3}$ (within $0 < t < 4$)

$\pi t = \pi, 3\pi$ (within $0 < t < 4$)
$t = 1, 3$ **A1**

Question:
The displacement, $s$, in metres, of a particle $t$ seconds after it passes through the origin is given by the expression $s = \ln(2 - e^{-t})$, $t \geq 0$.
(a) Find an expression for the velocity, $v$, of the particle at time $t$. [2]
(b) Find an expression for the acceleration, $a$, of the particle at time $t$. [2]
(c) Find the acceleration of the particle at time $t = 0$. [1]

Solution:
(a)
Velocity is the derivative of displacement with respect to time:
$v = \frac{ds}{dt} = \frac{d}{dt}[\ln(2 - e^{-t})]$ **M1**
Using the chain rule:
$v = \frac{1}{2 - e^{-t}} \cdot \frac{d}{dt}(2 - e^{-t}) = \frac{1}{2 - e^{-t}} \cdot e^{-t} = \frac{e^{-t}}{2 - e^{-t}}$ **A1**

(b)
Acceleration is the derivative of velocity with respect to time:
$a = \frac{dv}{dt} = \frac{d}{dt}\left(\frac{e^{-t}}{2 - e^{-t}}\right)$ **M1**
Using the quotient rule:
$a = \frac{(-e^{-t})(2 - e^{-t}) - (e^{-t})(e^{-t})}{(2 - e^{-t})^2} = \frac{-2e^{-t} + e^{-2t} - e^{-2t}}{(2 - e^{-t})^2} = \frac{-2e^{-t}}{(2 - e^{-t})^2}$ **A1**

(c)
Substitute $t = 0$ into the acceleration expression:
$a(0) = \frac{-2e^{0}}{(2 - e^{0})^2} = \frac{-2}{(2 - 1)^2} = \frac{-2}{1} = -2$ **A1**
The acceleration at time $t = 0$ is $-2$ ms$^{-2}$.

Question:
Xavier, the parachutist, jumps out of a plane at a height of $h$ metres above the ground. After free falling for 10 seconds his parachute opens. His velocity, $v$ ms$^{-1}$, $t$ seconds after jumping from the plane, can be modelled by the function
$v(t) = 
\begin{cases} 
9.8t, & 0 \leq t \leq 10 \\ 
\frac{98}{\sqrt{1 + (t - 10)^2}}, & t > 10 
\end{cases}$
His velocity when he reaches the ground is $2.8$ ms$^{-1}$.
(a) Find his velocity when $t = 15$. [2]
(b) Calculate the vertical distance Xavier travelled in the first 10 seconds. [2]
(c) Determine the value of $h$. [5]

Solution:
(a)
For $t = 15 > 10$, use the second case:
$v(15) = \frac{98}{\sqrt{1 + (15 - 10)^2}} = \frac{98}{\sqrt{1 + 25}} = \frac{98}{\sqrt{26}}$ **M1**
$v(15) = 19.2$ ms$^{-1}$ **A1**

(b)
Distance traveled in first 10 seconds:
$s = \displaystyle\int_0^{10} v(t)  dt = \displaystyle\int_0^{10} 9.8t  dt$ **M1**
$s = \left[4.9t^2\right]_0^{10} = 4.9 \times 100 = 490$ m **A1**

(c)
First, find the time $T$ when Xavier reaches the ground (when $v(T) = 2.8$ ms$^{-1}$):
$\frac{98}{\sqrt{1 + (T - 10)^2}} = 2.8$ **M1**
Solve for $T$:
$\sqrt{1 + (T - 10)^2} = \frac{98}{2.8} = 35$
$1 + (T - 10)^2 = 1225$
$(T - 10)^2 = 1224$
$T - 10 = \sqrt{1224} = 34.985...$
$T = 44.985$ seconds **A1**

Now calculate total distance fallen:
$h = \displaystyle\int_0^{10} 9.8t  dt + \displaystyle\int_{10}^{44.985} \frac{98}{\sqrt{1 + (t - 10)^2}}  dt$ **M1A1**
The first integral is 490 m (from part b).
For the second integral, use substitution $u = t - 10$:
$\displaystyle\int_{0}^{34.985} \frac{98}{\sqrt{1 + u^2}}  du = 98[\sinh^{-1} u]_0^{34.985} = 98\sinh^{-1}(34.985)$
$\sinh^{-1}(34.985) \approx \ln(34.985 + \sqrt{34.985^2 + 1}) \approx \ln(69.97) \approx 4.248$
Thus second distance $\approx 98 \times 4.248 \approx 416.3$ m
Total height $h \approx 490 + 416.3 = 906.3$ m
$h = 906$ m **A1**

$2e^{-t} = 2$
$e^{-t} = 1$

A particle moves in a straight line in a positive direction from a fixed point O. The velocity $v$ m s$^{-1}$, at time $t$ seconds, where $t \geq 0$, satisfies the differential equation
$
\frac{dv}{dt} = -\frac{v(1 + v^2)}{50}.
$
The particle starts from O with an initial velocity of $10$ m s$^{-1}$.
(a) 
$\quad$(i) Express as a definite integral, the time taken for the particle's velocity to decrease from $10$ m s$^{-1}$ to $5$ m s$^{-1}$. [3]
$\quad$(ii) Hence calculate the time taken for the particle's velocity to decrease from $10$ m s$^{-1}$ to $5$ m s$^{-1}$. [2]
(b) 
$\quad$(i) Show that, when $v > 0$, the motion of this particle can also be described by the differential equation $\frac{dv}{dx} = -\frac{(1 + v^2)}{50}$ where $x$ metres is the displacement from O. [2]
$\quad$(ii) Given that $v = 10$ when $x = 0$, solve the differential equation expressing $x$ in terms of $v$. [6]
$\quad$(iii) Hence show that $v = \frac{10 - \tan \frac{x}{50}}{1 + 10 \tan \frac{x}{50}}$. [4]

Separate variables:
$
\frac{dt}{dv} = -\frac{50}{v(1 + v^2)}
$
The time taken is:
$
t = \displaystyle\int_{10}^{5} \frac{dt}{dv}  dv = \displaystyle\int_{10}^{5} -\frac{50}{v(1 + v^2)}  dv = 50 \displaystyle\int_{5}^{10} \frac{1}{v(1 + v^2)}  dv
$
**M1A1A1**

(a)(ii)
Evaluate the integral:
$
\displaystyle\int \frac{1}{v(1 + v^2)}  dv = \ln \left( \frac{v}{\sqrt{1 + v^2}} \right) + C
$
Thus,
$
t = 50 \left[ \ln \left( \frac{v}{\sqrt{1 + v^2}} \right) \right]_{5}^{10} = 50 \left( \ln \left( \frac{10}{\sqrt{101}} \right) - \ln \left( \frac{5}{\sqrt{26}} \right) \right) = 50 \ln \left( \frac{10 \sqrt{26}}{5 \sqrt{101}} \right) = 50 \ln \left( \frac{2 \sqrt{26}}{\sqrt{101}} \right) = 50 \ln \left( \sqrt{\frac{104}{101}} \right) = 25 \ln \left( \frac{104}{101} \right)
$

(b)(i)
Acceleration can be written as $a = \frac{dv}{dt} = v \frac{dv}{dx}$. So,
$
v \frac{dv}{dx} = -\frac{v(1 + v^2)}{50}
$
Since $v > 0$, divide both sides by $v$:
$
\frac{dv}{dx} = -\frac{(1 + v^2)}{50}
$
**M1A1**

(b)(ii)
Separate variables:
$
\frac{dv}{1 + v^2} = -\frac{1}{50} dx
$
Integrate both sides:
$
\displaystyle\int \frac{dv}{1 + v^2} = -\frac{1}{50} \displaystyle\int dx
$
$
\arctan v = -\frac{x}{50} + C
$
**M1A1A1**
Apply initial condition $x = 0$, $v = 10$:
$
\arctan 10 = C
$

$
\arctan v = \arctan 10 - \frac{x}{50}
$
Solve for $x$:
$
x = 50 (\arctan 10 - \arctan v)
$
**M1A1**

(b)(iii)

$
v = \tan \left( \arctan 10 - \frac{x}{50} \right)
$
Use the identity $\tan(A - B) = \frac{\tan A - \tan B}{1 + \tan A \tan B}$:
$
v = \frac{\tan(\arctan 10) - \tan(\frac{x}{50})}{1 + \tan(\arctan 10) \tan(\frac{x}{50})} = \frac{10 - \tan(\frac{x}{50})}{1 + 10 \tan(\frac{x}{50})}
$
**M1A1M1A1**

Question:
A particle can move along a straight line from a point $O$. The velocity $v$, in ms$^{-1}$, is given by the function $v(t) = 1 - e^{-\sin t^2}$ where time $t \geq 0$ is measured in seconds.
(a) Write down the first two times $t_1, t_2 > 0$, when the particle changes direction. [2]
(b) (i) Find the time $t < t_2$ when the particle has a maximum velocity. [2]
    (ii) Find the time $t < t_2$ when the particle has a minimum velocity. [2]
(c) Find the distance travelled by the particle between times $t = t_1$ and $t = t_2$. [2]

Solution:
(a)
The particle changes direction when $v(t) = 0$. Solve $1 - e^{-\sin t^2} = 0$:
$e^{-\sin t^2} = 1 \Rightarrow \sin t^2 = 0 \Rightarrow t^2 = n\pi$, $n \in \mathbb{Z}^+$ **M1**
Thus, $t_1 = \sqrt{\pi} \approx 1.77$ s and $t_2 = \sqrt{2\pi} \approx 2.51$ s. **A1A1**

(b)(i)
Maximum velocity occurs when acceleration $a(t) = \frac{dv}{dt} = 0$ and $\frac{d^2v}{dt^2} < 0$.
$a(t) = \frac{d}{dt}(1 - e^{-\sin t^2}) = -e^{-\sin t^2} \cdot (-\cos t^2 \cdot 2t) = 2t \cos t^2 \cdot e^{-\sin t^2}$ **M1**
Set $a(t) = 0$: $2t \cos t^2 \cdot e^{-\sin t^2} = 0 \Rightarrow \cos t^2 = 0$ (since $t > 0$ and $e^{-\sin t^2} \neq 0$).
Thus, $t^2 = \frac{\pi}{2} \Rightarrow t = \sqrt{\frac{\pi}{2}} \approx 1.25$ s. **A1**

(b)(ii)
Minimum velocity occurs when $a(t) = 0$ and $\frac{d^2v}{dt^2} > 0$.
Next solution: $t^2 = \frac{3\pi}{2} \Rightarrow t = \sqrt{\frac{3\pi}{2}} \approx 2.17$ s. **M1A1**

(c)
Distance travelled between $t_1$ and $t_2$:
Since $v(t) < 0$ in this interval (as $\sin t^2$ is negative between $\pi$ and $2\pi$), distance = $\displaystyle\int_{t_1}^{t_2} |v(t)|  dt = \displaystyle\int_{\sqrt{\pi}}^{\sqrt{2\pi}} (e^{-\sin t^2} - 1)  dt$ **M1**
Numerical integration gives distance $\approx 0.711$ m. **A1**

Question:
[This question may require knowledge from the later chapter: differential equation]
The acceleration of a car is $\frac{1}{40} (60 - v)$ ms$^{-2}$, when its velocity is $v$ ms$^{-1}$. Given the car starts from rest, find the velocity of the car after 30 seconds. [6]

Solution:
METHOD 1
Acceleration is given by $\frac{dv}{dt} = \frac{1}{40}(60 - v)$ **M1**
Separate variables:
$\displaystyle\int \frac{dv}{60 - v} = \displaystyle\int \frac{dt}{40}$ **M1**
Integrate both sides:
$-\ln|60 - v| = \frac{t}{40} + C$ **A1**
Apply initial condition: at $t = 0$, $v = 0$:
$-\ln 60 = C$ **A1**
Thus, $-\ln(60 - v) = \frac{t}{40} - \ln 60$
Solve for $v$ when $t = 30$: **M1**
$\ln(60 - v) = \ln 60 - \frac{30}{40} = \ln 60 - \frac{3}{4}$
$60 - v = 60 e^{-\frac{3}{4}}$
$v = 60 - 60 e^{-\frac{3}{4}} = 60(1 - e^{-0.75})$
$v = 31.7$ ms$^{-1}$ **A1**

METHOD 2
From $\frac{dv}{dt} = \frac{1}{40}(60 - v)$, we have $\frac{dt}{dv} = \frac{40}{60 - v}$ **M1**
Integrate from $v=0$ to $v=v_f$:
$\displaystyle\int_0^{v_f} \frac{40}{60 - v}  dv = \displaystyle\int_0^{30} dt = 30$ **A1A1**
Solve the integral:
$40 \displaystyle\int_0^{v_f} \frac{dv}{60 - v} = 40[-\ln|60 - v|]_0^{v_f} = 40(-\ln(60 - v_f) + \ln 60) = 40 \ln \left( \frac{60}{60 - v_f} \right)$
Set equal to 30:
$40 \ln \left( \frac{60}{60 - v_f} \right) = 30$ **M1**
$\ln \left( \frac{60}{60 - v_f} \right) = \frac{3}{4}$
$\frac{60}{60 - v_f} = e^{0.75}$
$60 - v_f = 60 e^{-0.75}$
$v_f = 60(1 - e^{-0.75}) = 31.7$ ms$^{-1}$ **A1**
